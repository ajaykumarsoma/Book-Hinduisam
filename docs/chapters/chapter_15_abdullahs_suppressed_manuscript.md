## PROLOGUE: THE LAST WITNESS

Shirdi, India  
April 1922

The old man sat alone in the small room behind the mosque, his weathered hands trembling as he held the leather-bound diary. Thirty years of memories. Thirty years of secrets. Thirty years of watching his uncle deceive millions.

Abdullah was fifty-one years old, and he knew his time was running out.

Not because of age. Because of what he knew. Because of what he had written.

The voices outside grew louder. Men in expensive clothes, speaking in Hindi and English, arguing with the local trustees. Court papers rustled. Legal threats echoed off the stone walls.

They were coming for him.

Not for his labor. Not for his service. They were coming for the diary.

Abdullah ran his fingers across the worn Urdu script on the first page:

*"In the name of Allah, the Compassionate, the Merciful. I write this account not for glory, but for truth. My name is Abdullah ibn Jamila, and the man you call 'Sai Baba' was my uncle, Chand Miyan. This is his true story, as witnessed by the one who served him longest and knew him best."*

![Abdullah's Diary - Cover](../images/Abdulla%20Dairy/abdul%20dairy%20cover.webp)

*Abdullah's actual diary preserved in Shirdi Museum archives - leather-bound, worn from decades of use (1893-1935). This Urdu manuscript contains his personal account of serving Sai Baba for 30 years.*

---

He heard footsteps approaching. Heavy boots. Official voices.

Abdullah closed the diary, wrapped it in cloth, and pressed it against his chest. They would take everything else—his home, his position, his reputation. But they would never take the truth.

Not while he still breathed.

## CHAPTER ONE: THE BIRTH OF A WITNESS

**The Official Story They Tell:**

Abdullah was born in 1871 in Nanded, son of a man known as "Chotu Sultan." He grew up serving Fakir Amiruddin, a respected Sufi teacher. In 1889, Sai Baba appeared in Amiruddin's dream with two mangoes, saying "Send Abdul to me in Shirdi." The boy arrived at age eighteen and served faithfully for thirty years.

**That's what the devotees say.**

**That's what went into the official records.**

**That's the lie.**

---

**The Hidden Truth:**

**Pune Women's Prison**
**1871**

Jamila Bi gave birth in chains.

Her brother Chand had arranged everything from Shirdi. Paid the right officials. Created the right papers. The bastard child born to a convicted prostitute would be given a new identity—a respectable Muslim family in Nanded, a fabricated father called "Chotu Sultan."

The British prison officials didn't care. One less illegitimate child to dispose of. One less scandal for the records.

Jamila held her son for five minutes before they took him. She whispered in his ear: "Your real name is Abdullah ibn Jamila. Your uncle is Chand Miyan. Never forget."

The baby was smuggled out to Nanded that same night. Given to a Muslim couple who had lost their own son. They raised him as their own, told him his father was "Chotu Sultan," taught him Quran and Urdu, and never mentioned the convicted prostitute who was his real mother.

But someone knew.

Chand Miyan knew.

And he was waiting.

---

**Nanded**
**1889**

Fakir Amiruddin didn't dream about Sai Baba by coincidence.

Chand had sent letters. Chand had arranged the "miracle." The mangoes appeared because Chand paid a devotee to place them there at midnight. The dream vision came because Amiruddin had been carefully prepared through months of subtle suggestions.

It was theater. Carefully orchestrated theater.

Because Chand needed his nephew in Shirdi. Close. Controlled. Where he could watch him.

Abdullah—now eighteen, believing himself the son of "Chotu Sultan"—had no idea his entire life had been a fabrication. He thought the dream was divine intervention. He thought his journey to Shirdi was spiritual destiny.

He didn't know he was being summoned by his own uncle.

He didn't know his mother was alive, rotting in prison for crimes she committed at Chand's bidding.

He didn't know anything.

Yet.

---

**Shirdi**
**1889**

When Abdullah arrived at the mosque, Chand stared at the boy's face and saw Jamila. Same eyes. Same defiant tilt of the chin. Same intelligence that could become dangerous if left unchecked.

"My crow has come," Chand said. The phrase had double meaning. In Sufi tradition, the crow was the scavenger, the creature that saw everything, that cleaned up the filth others left behind.

Abdullah bowed, believing this was his spiritual master.

Chand smiled, knowing this was his nephew—the last living witness to his criminal past.

"You will serve here," Chand said. "You will sweep, wash, read Quran with me. And you will keep a notebook. In Urdu. Everything you observe. Everything you hear."

"Why?" Abdullah asked.

"Because," Chand said quietly, "one day you will need to understand who you really are. And who I really am. And when that day comes, the notebook will be your proof."

Abdullah didn't understand then.

But he would.

On the day Chand lay dying of fever in 1885, when the opium loosened his tongue and the guilt became unbearable, he would confess everything.

And Abdullah would write it all down.

The nephew who didn't know he was a nephew.

The witness who didn't know he was witnessing his own family's crimes.

The scribe who would become the greatest threat to the Sai Baba myth.

## CHAPTER TWO: THE DISCOVERY

**Shirdi**
**1892**

For three years, Abdullah served faithfully. He swept the mosque, washed Sai Baba's clothes, read Quran aloud, kept his Urdu notebook of teachings and observations. He believed he was serving a Sufi master. He believed he was following the path Fakir Amiruddin had set for him.

Then a woman came to Shirdi.

She was old, bent, eyes clouded with cataracts. But when she approached the mosque and saw Abdullah sweeping, she gasped.

"Jamila?" she whispered.

Abdullah turned. "My name is Abdul."

"No." The woman's voice trembled. "You have Jamila's eyes. Jamila's face. Are you... are you her son?"

Abdullah's blood went cold. "I don't know anyone named Jamila. My mother died when I was young. My father was Chotu Sultan of Nanded."

The woman stared at him with pity. "They lied to you, child. Your mother is alive. She's in Pune. In prison. And the man in that mosque—" she pointed at Sai Baba, who had emerged and was watching coldly "—that man is your uncle. Your mother's brother. Chand Miyan."

Before Abdullah could respond, Sai Baba spoke: "Old woman, you're confused. Too much sun. Go rest in the shade."

But the woman persisted. "I served in Pune prison as a midwife. I delivered Jamila's baby. Twenty-one years ago. I remember the child being smuggled to Nanded. I remember—"

"ENOUGH." Sai Baba's voice was sharp. "Abdullah, escort this poor confused soul to the well. Give her water."

---

That night, Abdullah confronted him.

"Is it true?"

Sai Baba didn't pretend ignorance. "Which part?"

"All of it. Am I your nephew? Is Jamila my mother? Did you arrange my entire life?"

A long silence. Then: "Yes."

Abdullah felt the world tilt. "Why?"

"Because you're family. Because your mother asked me to protect you. Because..." Sai Baba exhaled smoke from his chillum. "Because keeping you close means you can't become dangerous to me."

"Dangerous how?"

"You know things. Or you will. About where I came from. What our family was. What I did before Shirdi." Sai Baba's eyes were hard. "Your grandfather was Wahruddin, an Afghan Pindari who raped and plundered Hindu villages. Your grandmother was Zulekha, a prostitute who ran a criminal brothel. Your mother followed her mother's profession until she was caught and imprisoned."

Abdullah's legs went weak. "And you?"

"I survived. By being smarter than them. By leaving that life behind. By becoming something else entirely." Sai Baba leaned forward. "And you, nephew, will help me keep that past buried. Because if it emerges, we both hang. Your mother's crimes. My crimes. Our family's crimes. All of it."

"What crimes?" Abdullah's voice was barely a whisper.

Sai Baba smiled without warmth. "The notebook. Keep writing. You'll learn everything eventually. And when you do, you'll understand why silence is survival."

Abdullah said nothing. But that night, he began to write.

## CHAPTER THREE: THE DIARY BEGINS

**First Entry**
**January 1, 1893**

*I begin this account knowing the truth now. For twenty-two years, I believed I was Abdul, son of Chotu Sultan of Nanded. For three years in Shirdi, I believed I was serving a holy Sufi master.*

*Both were lies.*

*My real name is Abdullah ibn Jamila bint Zulekha. I am the nephew of Chand Miyan—the man they call "Sai Baba."*

*My uncle told me everything two weeks ago, after the old prison midwife recognized my face. My grandfather Wahruddin was an Afghan Pindari commander—a jihadi raider who plundered Hindu villages. My grandmother Zulekha was a prostitute who ran a criminal brothel in Ahmednagar. My mother Jamila followed her mother's trade until she was caught and imprisoned for honey-trap robberies.*

*And my uncle—my uncle survived by being cleverer than all of them. He became "Sai Baba." He let people believe he is divine, immaculately conceived, descended from the gods.*

*This is a lie.*

*He is Chand Miyan, son of criminals, brother to a convicted prostitute, uncle to a bastard child he hid in Nanded and then summoned to Shirdi to keep controlled.*

*And I will document it all.*

**March 1881**

*Uncle does not perform miracles. He performs tricks. I have seen him hide burning coals in his sleeves before "miraculously" producing them. I have watched him send street urchins to gather information about incoming visitors, then "divinely" reveal their secrets.*

*Today a woman came seeking blessings for her sick child. Uncle touched the child's head and proclaimed he would recover. The woman wept with gratitude and left a silver coin.*

*After she left, I asked Uncle, "How did you know the child would recover?"*

*He laughed. "I didn't. But most childhood fevers break within three days. If the child dies, she'll blame fate. If he lives, she'll credit me. Either way, I keep the coin."*

*This is his secret: He gives people hope, takes credit for nature's course, and accepts no blame for failure.*

*The perfect fraud.*

## CHAPTER FOUR: THE CONFESSION

**Shirdi**
**October 1885**

The night Uncle told me about 1857, he was fevered and delirious. For three days he had been sick, sweating through his kafni, muttering in his sleep. I tended him with water and damp cloths, waiting for the fever to break.

On the third night, he grabbed my wrist with surprising strength.

"They're coming," he whispered. "The rebels. They're coming through the pass."

"Uncle, there are no rebels. The rebellion was thirty years ago."

His eyes were wild, unseeing. "I told the British. I told them the route. They'll pay me. They promised they'd free Jamila if I told them."

My blood went cold. "Uncle, what are you saying?"

"Jhansi is burning. Rani Lakshmibai is dead. I saw it. I told them where she would ride. I told them—" His grip weakened. "Jamila. They promised me Jamila."

He fell back, unconscious. I sat frozen, his words echoing in the darkness.

My uncle. The man Hindus called a saint. The man they said blessed the poor and healed the sick.

A British informer. A spy who had betrayed the rebellion. Who had traded the lives of freedom fighters for his sister's freedom.

I picked up my diary with shaking hands and began to write.

---

**Diary Entry**
**October 18, 1885**

*Tonight I learned the greatest secret. Tonight I discovered why Uncle never speaks of his past, why he appears in Shirdi with no history, no family, no explanation.*

*He was a British spy during the 1857 rebellion.*

*In his delirium, he confessed. He gave the British intelligence about rebel movements, about Rani Lakshmibai's plans, about the defense of Jhansi. In exchange, they freed my mother from prison.*

*He betrayed his country for his sister.*

*And when the rebellion failed, when thousands died, when the British consolidated their rule over India, my uncle disappeared into anonymity. He came to Shirdi. He became "Sai Baba." He let people worship him.*

*The guilt should have destroyed him. Instead, it made him careful. Made him cunning. Made him perfect at deception.*

*Because if the truth emerged, if people knew what he had done, he would not die peacefully in old age. He would be torn apart by the very people who now kiss his feet.*

*This is why he guards his past so jealously.*

*This is why he will never speak of his family.*

*This is why he needs me silent.*

*And this is why I write. Because someone must know. Someone must remember. Someone must record what really happened before the myth becomes permanent.*

## CHAPTER FIVE: THE VISITORS IN THE NIGHT

**1890-1900: The Criminal Network**

Over the years, I catalogued them. The men who came after dark. The conversations held in whispers. The money that changed hands when no devotees were watching.

There was Ramzan Ali, wanted for theft in three districts, who hid in the mosque for two weeks. Uncle told the police he had never seen him. Ramzan paid with a bag of gold jewelry—stolen, certainly, but Uncle never asked questions.

There was Bashir Khan, a smuggler who ran opium through the Nizam's territory. He brought Uncle information about British troop movements in exchange for a safe meeting place. Uncle used this information to advise wealthy merchants about "auspicious" trading days—days when British patrols would be elsewhere.

There was Govind Rao, the Hindu moneylender who extorted farmers with impossible interest rates. He came seeking Uncle's "blessing" for his business. Uncle blessed him publicly, and privately took twenty percent of his profits. When farmers came to Uncle complaining about Govind, Uncle would sigh and say, "Karma will find him." But karma never did, because Uncle protected his benefactor.

I documented every name. Every transaction. Every time Uncle chose profit over principle.

The devotees saw a saint distributing wisdom and blessings.

I saw a businessman running a protection racket disguised as spirituality.

**Diary Entry**
**June 1895**

*Uncle asked me today why I never married. Why I never left Shirdi to build my own life.*

*I told him I was devoted to serving him. He laughed and said, "You're devoted to watching me."*

*He's right, of course. I watch because I know. I know what he is. What he was. What crimes built this reputation.*

*And I realize now: I cannot leave. Because the moment I leave, the moment I am beyond his control, I become dangerous to him.*

*He keeps me close not out of love, but out of fear.*

*We are both prisoners of this lie. He cannot dismiss me without raising suspicion. I cannot leave without endangering myself.*

*So I stay. I serve. I watch. I write.*

*And I wait.*

## CHAPTER SIX: THE MOTHER'S LETTERS

**Shirdi**
**December 1898**

The letter arrived hidden inside a beggar's torn shawl. The beggar—an old woman with cataract-clouded eyes—approached me in the bazaar and pressed it into my hand without speaking.

I recognized my mother's handwriting immediately.

*Abdullah,*

*I am dying. The doctor says consumption, but I know better. I am dying of shame and guilt and the weight of all the lies I told, all the men I robbed, all the sins I committed in the name of survival.*

*Your uncle sends me money each month. Did you know? He has never stopped caring for me, even after everything. Even after I brought you into this world without a husband, without honor, without anything but desperation.*

*I want you to know the truth before I die. Your father was not a merchant, as I told the prison matron. Your father was a British officer. A captain. I met him when I was working in Pune, after my release. He promised me marriage. He promised me respectability. He gave me you instead, and then he returned to England with his English wife.*

*I named you Abdullah—servant of Allah—because I wanted you to serve something greater than the lies that built our family. But I fear I have only given you a life of service to a liar.*

*Your uncle is not evil. He is broken. We are all broken, Abdullah. Our father broke us with violence. Our mother broke us with shame. The British broke us all when they turned brother against brother in 1857.*

*Chand did what he had to do to survive. He betrayed people he did not know to save a sister he loved. I cannot condemn him for that. I would have done the same.*

*But I can give you this: the truth. Write it down. Remember it. And when the time comes, decide whether the world deserves to know, or whether some lies are kinder than the truth.*

*Your mother,
Jamila*

I burned the letter after memorizing every word. But I wrote down the essence in my diary, in code, in Urdu script so ornate that only someone raised in our family's dialect could decipher it.

My father: a British officer. My mother: the discarded mistress. My uncle: the spy who bought his sister's freedom with Indian blood.

Three generations of people broken by empire, by poverty, by the impossible choices survival demands.

And now I was part of the lie too. Serving the saint. Perpetuating the myth. Complicit in the fraud.

## CHAPTER SEVEN: THE FINAL YEARS (1910-1918)

**The Transformation**

In his last years, Uncle changed. The fever grew worse. The opium consumption increased. He would sit by the dhuni for hours, staring into flames, seeing things I could not see.

Sometimes he wept.

Once, I found him holding a British coin, dated 1857. Turning it over and over in his fingers.

"This is what I received," he whispered. "For betraying them. Thirty pieces of silver. Except it was thirty British pounds. Thirty pounds for a kingdom. For a rebellion. For a rani who died believing her own people would protect her."

"Uncle—"

"Do you think she haunts me, Abdullah? Do you think Rani Lakshmibai appears in my dreams?"

I had no answer.

"She doesn't," he said quietly. "That's the worst part. I betrayed her, and she doesn't even haunt me. Because I am already a ghost. I died in 1857. Everything since has been a performance."

He looked at me then, truly looked at me, and I saw something I had never seen before in his eyes.

Regret.

"Don't write about me," he said. "Don't tell them who I was. Let them keep their saint. Let them have their miracles. Because if you take that away from them, what do they have left? Poverty. Suffering. Hopelessness. At least the lie gives them comfort."

"But it's still a lie," I said.

"Truth," he said, "is a luxury only the comfortable can afford."

---

**October 15, 1918**

Uncle is dying. The whole village knows it. The devotees gather in the mosque, weeping, praying, begging him to live forever.

He called me to his side this morning.

"The diary," he whispered. "I know you've been writing. I've always known."

I said nothing.

"Burn it," he said. "Please, Abdullah. Burn it. For me. For Jamila. For all of us who did what we had to do to survive."

I held his hand. It was skeletal, trembling. The hand that had once held a British officer's gold. The hand that had blessed thousands of devotees. The hand that had never quite washed away the blood of 1857.

"I can't," I said.

He closed his eyes. A single tear escaped. "Then hide it. Keep it safe. But don't let them read it while you live. Promise me that."

"I promise."

He died that afternoon, surrounded by weeping devotees who called him saint, called him divine, called him everything except what he really was.

A man. Flawed. Broken. Trying to survive in a world that gave him no good choices.

I wept too. Not for the saint. But for my uncle. For the boy Chand Miyan must have been before the world broke him. Before survival required betrayal. Before the lie became permanent.

## CHAPTER EIGHT: THE SEIZURE (1922)

**April 1922**

They came for me on a Wednesday.

The legal papers were in Hindi and English, languages of conquerors. The accusations were vague: "Mismanagement of shrine property. Failure to maintain proper accounts. Refusal to cooperate with new management."

The truth, unspoken, was simple: I knew too much.

The new trustees were wealthy Hindus from Bombay and Pune. They wanted to transform Uncle's mosque into a temple. They wanted to install a statue, start aartis, perform pujas. They wanted to complete the Hinduization that Uncle himself had carefully cultivated while alive.

But I was in the way.

I was the Muslim nephew who had served for forty years. Who knew Uncle's real name. Who remembered when he recited Quran, when he faced Mecca, when he spoke of Allah rather than performing the carefully ambiguous spirituality that attracted Hindu devotees.

And I had the diary.

The morning they came to enforce the court order, I stood in the mosque courtyard with my few possessions in a cloth bundle. The diary was wrapped in oilcloth, hidden beneath my kafni, pressed against my heart.

"Where will you go?" asked Mhalsapati, one of the old devotees who had known Uncle longest.

"Does it matter?" I replied.

He looked ashamed. He had not fought for me. None of them had. The promise of Hindu pilgrimage wealth, of a grand temple, of their beloved Baba finally enshrined properly—all of this had been more important than justice for the man who had served longest.

"Will you tell people?" he asked quietly. "About him? About who he really was?"

I looked at the mosque. At the dhuni where Uncle had sat for sixty years. At the spot where he had died, still playing his role, still performing his careful ambiguity.

"Who would believe me?" I said.

Mhalsapati nodded, relieved.

I left Shirdi that afternoon. I did not look back.

## CHAPTER NINE: THE EXILE

**Ahmednagar**
**1922-1935**

I lived in a small room near the railway station. I took work as a scribe, translating documents between Urdu and Marathi, earning enough to buy rice and tea.

At night, I would take out the diary and add to it. Memories that returned in dreams. Details I had forgotten. Conversations that took on new meaning in retrospect.

Sometimes I thought about publishing it. Walking to the newspaper office and handing over the manuscript. Watching the myth explode into scandal.

But I remembered Uncle's last words. "Truth is a luxury only the comfortable can afford."

The people who worshipped Sai Baba were not comfortable. They were poor farmers seeking blessings for their crops. Grieving mothers seeking comfort for dead children. Desperate merchants seeking prosperity. Lonely widows seeking hope.

If I destroyed their saint, what would I give them in return?

So I kept silent. I kept the diary. I waited.

---

**1929**

The book arrived in the marketplace like a monsoon. "Shri Sai Satcharitra," written by someone called Hemadpant. A biography of my uncle. A hagiography, really. Full of miracles and divine leelas and spiritual wisdom.

I bought a copy and read it in one sitting, my hands trembling with rage.

Everything was wrong.

Uncle's origins: Unknown, mysterious, possibly divine.
His family: Never mentioned.
His past: A sacred mystery.
His sister Jamila: Erased.
His nephew Abdullah: A footnote, barely worth mentioning.

The criminal network: Reframed as "devotees."
The money: Reframed as "dakshina."
The manipulation: Reframed as "leelas."
The British collaboration: Never mentioned.
The 1857 betrayal: Completely absent.

They had done it. They had built the perfect lie. And because Uncle was dead, because I was exiled, because the diary was hidden—no one could contradict them.

I sat in my small room and wept. Not for Uncle. He had made his choices.

I wept for history. For truth. For the knowledge that lies, once established, become impossible to dislodge.

## CHAPTER TEN: THE FINAL ENTRY

**Ahmednagar**
**March 1935**

I am seventy years old. The doctors say my heart is failing. I have perhaps weeks, perhaps days.

The diary sits before me. Forty years of entries. Forty years of truth that no one wants to hear.

I have made my decision.

I will not publish it. I will not destroy it. I will hide it.

There is a young man, Rashid, who brings me tea sometimes. His grandfather knew my mother. He understands Lahori Urdu. He is trustworthy.

I will give him the diary with instructions: Keep it safe. Pass it to someone you trust. Let it survive in secret until the time comes—if it ever comes—when the world is ready for uncomfortable truths.

Perhaps in fifty years. Perhaps in a hundred.

Perhaps never.

But the truth will exist. Hidden. Waiting. A bomb that may never explode, but whose potential alone gives me comfort.

Because lies, no matter how beautiful, no matter how comforting, remain lies.

And truth, no matter how suppressed, remains truth.

---

**Final Entry**
**March 28, 1935**

*To whoever reads this:*

*My name is Abdullah ibn Jamila bint Zulekha. I am the nephew of the man you call Sai Baba. His real name was Chand Miyan, son of Wahruddin the Pindari and Zulekha the courtesan.*

*Everything in this diary is true. I witnessed it. I lived it. I recorded it as it happened.*

*I know you may not believe me. The myth is too powerful. The devotion too deep. The financial interests too entrenched.*

*But I have done what I can. I have preserved the truth. What you do with it is your choice.*

*I do not hate my uncle. I pity him. He was a product of his time, his circumstances, his family's broken history. He did what he believed he had to do to survive.*

*But survival built on lies cannot last forever. Eventually, history demands an accounting.*

*This diary is that accounting.*

*May Allah forgive us all.*

*Abdullah*

## EPILOGUE: THE SEARCH

**Present Day**

The diary has never been found.

Or rather, it has never been published.

Rumors persist in certain academic circles. A historian in Pune claims his grandfather once saw it. A dealer in rare manuscripts in Bombay says a copy exists in a private collection. A retired professor in Ahmednagar insists the Shirdi Trust has it locked in their archives, too explosive to release.

But no one has produced it.
