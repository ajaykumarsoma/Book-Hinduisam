## Part 1: Kevin R. D. Shepherd — Exposing Sectarian Bias and Hinduization

### Who Is Kevin R. D. Shepherd?

**British historian and independent scholar** who:
- Conducted 12 years of private research at Cambridge University Library (1981-1993)
- Published *Gurus Rediscovered* (1986) — first non-sectarian comparison of Shirdi Sai and Upasani Maharaj
- Published *Sai Baba of Shirdi: A Biographical Investigation* (2015) — 400+ page historical analysis
- Criticized both Hindu sectarians and Sathya Sai devotees for distorting history

**Methodology**: Historical documentation, textual criticism, analysis of primary sources in multiple languages

### Key Findings by Shepherd

#### **Finding 1: Sai Baba Was a Muslim Fakir, Not Hindu**

**Evidence cited by Shepherd:**

1. **Early British Report (1911)**:  
   > "The report describes Sai Baba as a faqir, adding the words 'said to be a Mahomedan'... 'an old man of about 70 who came to Shirdi some 30 or 35 years ago and put up in the village mosque where he has resided since.'"  
   > — British policing department report, Calcutta (Shepherd 2015:22)

2. **Islamic Attire & Language**:  
   > "His attire and also his habit of speaking in Deccani Urdu... He is reported to have frequently uttered Islamic phrases, especially **Allah Malik** (God is the Owner/Ruler)."  
   > — Shepherd, kevinrdshepherd.info

3. **Abdul Baba's Urdu Notebook**:  
   > "The Notebook largely pertains to Muslim and Sufi material in Deccani Urdu; there are a number of quotations in Arabic included from the Quran and hadith... the fact that the manuscript's Islamic nature does not fit in with the accepted Hindu interpretation and presentation of Sai Baba may explain why it has remained unpublished [until 1999]."  
   > — Cited in Shepherd from Warren 1999

**Shepherd's Conclusion**:
Sai Baba was **"a liberal Muslim Sufi"** who accommodated Hindus but **never identified as Hindu**.

#### **Finding 2: The Hinduization Process — Systematic Erasure**

**Shepherd documents how Hindu devotees systematically Hinduized the Muslim fakir:**

**STEP 1: English Translation Distortion (N.V. Gunaji)**

> "The Marathi biography... composed by an early brahman devotee [Dabholkar] who repeatedly acknowledged and indicated the Muslim faqir identity of the saint. However, the **English adaptation by N. V. Gunaji neglected to include the Muslim context**, instead improvising a Vedantic complexion to the subject."
> — Shepherd, kevinrdshepherd.info

**What Gunaji deleted:**
- Frequent use of Urdu by Shirdi Sai
- Sections referring to Muslims, Muslim practices, and Sufi teachings
- Reference to Islamic ritual of goat slaughter (takkya)
- Context showing Sai Baba as Muslim

**STEP 2: The "Brahmin Parentage" Fabrication**

**Shepherd cites the Pathri legend as unverified:**

> "An influential theory of his Hindu birth at Pathri remains unconfirmed... The motif of the Hindu birth of reputed Muslim figures is often attested to in Indian hagiographic literature."
> — Shepherd 2015

**STEP 3: Narasimhaswami's Hagiography**

> "A prominent source is B. V. Narasimhaswami. This ascetic did not himself meet Sai Baba, not appearing at Shirdi until 1936 [18 years after death]. He contributed much valuable data, and also his own interpretations. Certain discrepancies have caused confusion... **A tendency to hagiography is evident.**"

**Shepherd's critique of Narasimhaswami:**

## Part 2: Dr. Marianne Warren — Proving Sai Baba Was a Sufi Mystic

### Who Is Dr. Marianne Warren?

**Canadian academic scholar** who:
- **Ph.D. in South Asian Studies**, University of Toronto (1996)
- Dissertation: *The Maharashtrian Sufi Context of Hazarat Sri Sai Baba of Shirdi (1838-1918)*
- Published *Unravelling the Enigma: Shirdi Sai Baba in the Light of Sufism* (1999, revised 2004)
- **First scholar to translate Abdul Baba's Urdu Notebook** (suppressed for 80+ years!)
- Conducted fieldwork in Maharashtra, interviewed Abdul's grandson

**Methodology**: Sufi textual analysis, Urdu/Arabic translation, comparative mysticism, field research

### Key Findings by Warren

#### **Finding 1: Abdul's Urdu Notebook — The Smoking Gun**

**Warren's breakthrough**: First English translation of Abdul Baba's notebook, revealing Sai Baba's **Islamic and Sufi teachings**.

**What the notebook contains:**

> "The manuscript largely pertains to **Muslim and Sufi material in Deccani Urdu**; there are a number of quotations in Arabic included from the **Quran and hadith** [traditions of the Prophet]... **the fact that the manuscript's Islamic nature does not fit in with the accepted Hindu interpretation** and presentation of Sai Baba may explain why it has remained unpublished."
> — Warren 1999:267

**Contents analysis:**

1. **Islamic History**: Early Shi'ism, Ismaili teachings
2. **Sufi Orders**: Discussions of tariqa (Sufi path)
3. **Quranic Commentary**: Abdul would read Quran, Sai Baba would comment
4. **Arabic Quotations**: Direct citations from hadith

**Warren's conclusion:**

> "The Notebook **establishes beyond doubt** that Sai Baba was **totally familiar with both the Islamic and Sufi traditions**, and that **as a Sufi master he taught this tradition to Abdul**."
> — Warren 1999:277

**This destroys the "Hindu saint" narrative completely.**

#### **Finding 2: The Bhagavad Gita Episode — Misinterpreted as "Sanskrit Knowledge"**

**Hindu hagiographers claim**: Sai Baba knew Sanskrit because he explained a Bhagavad Gita verse.

**Warren's demolition of this claim:**

> "Sai Baba's explanation of the Gita verse is **'totally different' to the version of Shankara and other canonical Hindu commentators**... the relevant dialogue does not in fact prove that Sai Baba knew the Gita or even Sanskrit, his emphasis being **Sufistic**."
> — Warren 1999:360

**What actually happened:**

1. Hindu devotee **read the verse aloud to Sai Baba**
2. Hindu devotee **explained the grammatical meanings**
3. Sai Baba gave a **unique Sufi interpretation** (not Vedantic)
4. Sai Baba's point: Guru teaches **ajnana (ignorance)** to remove ignorance, not jnana

**Warren's verdict:**

> "**Sai Baba had all the raw material of the verse given to him**, so there is no basis to the supposition that he in fact 'knew' Sanskrit or even the Bhagavad Gita."
> — Warren 1999:357

**Translation**: Hindus fed him the verse, he gave a Sufi spin. **Not Sanskrit mastery, but Sufi reinterpretation.**

#### **Finding 3: Narasimhaswami's Interview Bias — 43 Hindus vs 4 Muslims**

**Warren exposes systematic bias in Narasimhaswami's methodology:**

> "The interviews conducted by Narasimhaswami, during 1936, were reported in his work entitled *Devotees' Experiences of Sri Sai Baba*. **The majority of those persons interviewed became devotees of the Shirdi saint after 1910.** Only three of them had a link with Sai Baba prior to 1900."
> — Warren 1999:360

**Why this matters:**

1. **Post-1910 influx**: Urban Hindu devotees arrived late (last 8 years)
2. **Pre-1900 witnesses**: Only 3 interviewed = early period erased
3. **Muslim exclusion**: Narasimhaswami could not speak Urdu/Marathi fluently

**Warren's damning observation:**

> "As Narasimhaswami could not speak these languages, **he did not interview faqirs in the Shirdi environs who had known Sai Baba**. An important aspect of Sai Baba's activity was contact with the wandering faqirs, who spoke Marathi or Urdu."
> — Warren 1999:356

**Result**: The **Muslim and Sufi context was systematically excluded** from Narasimhaswami's hagiography.

#### **Finding 4: The "Allah Malik" Refrain — Islamic Identity Erased**

**Warren highlights what Hindu biographers minimize:**

> "Sai Baba's refrain of **Allah Malik** [God is the Master/Owner]. Dabholkar describes: 'He disliked any thought contrary to this assertion and **would not tolerate any dissent**.'"
> — Warren 1999:348-349

**Frequency of Islamic phrases:**
- "Allah Malik" (constantly)
- "Sabka Malik Ek" (One God is Master of All) — Islamic monotheism
- Quranic recitation daily
- Islamic burial customs followed

**Warren's conclusion:**

> "It was only after coming to terms with the evidence that **Sai Baba was, as consistently stated by his Hindu biographers, both a Muslim and a mystic — which by definition makes him a Sufi** — that his life and teachings began to fall into place."
> — Warren 1999

#### **Finding 5: The Deccani Sufi Context — 17th-18th Century Poet-Saints**

**Warren's unique contribution**: She translated works of Maharashtrian Sufi poet-saints (17th-18th centuries) whose **lives and teachings echo Sai Baba**.

**These Sufis:**
- Lived in Maharashtra under Nizam rule
- Combined Islamic mysticism with local vernacular
- Were tolerant toward Hindus (unlike orthodox Muslims)
- Used Urdu/Marathi poetry to teach Sufi concepts

**Warren's thesis:**

> "Their lives and writings echo the life and teachings of Sai Baba... the Maharashtrian Sufi Context of Hazarat Sri Sai Baba."
> — Warren, book description

**This places Sai Baba in a LONG TRADITION of Deccani Sufi mystics, NOT Hindu saints.**

### Warren's Academic Verdict

**Sai Baba was:**
1. ✅ A **Sufi mystic** (proved by Abdul's Notebook)
2. ✅ A **Muslim** (Islamic practices, Quranic knowledge, Allah Malik refrain)
3. ✅ Part of **Deccani Sufi tradition** (17th-18th century lineage)
4. ❌ **NOT a Hindu saint** (no Vedic knowledge, no Hindu practices, lived in mosque)
5. ❌ **NOT "both Hindu and Muslim"** (this is Hindu hagiographic overlay)

**Key Contribution**: Warren provided **academic rigor** and **linguistic evidence** (Urdu/Arabic sources) that Hindu hagiographers **suppressed for 80+ years**.

## Part 3: Professor David Hardiman — The Miracle-Worker as Nationalist Symbol

### Who Is Professor David Hardiman?

**British historian specializing in modern India** who:
- **Emeritus Professor**, Department of History, University of Warwick
- Founding member of **Subaltern Studies** group
- **D.Phil. in South Asian History**, University of Sussex (1975)
- Published *Miracle Cures for a Suffering Nation: Sai Baba of Shirdi* (2015) in *Comparative Studies in Society and History*

**Methodology**: Social history, nationalist ideology analysis, biopolitics, subaltern studies

### Key Findings by Hardiman

#### **Finding 1: The Miracle-Worker Persona as Modern Nationalist Symbol**

**Hardiman's thesis**: Sai Baba was **transformed from a regional figure into a pan-Indian symbol** to serve **nationalist ideology**.

**From the abstract:**

> "The miracle doctor became a means to reveal the **limited scope of Western science and culture**... I study Sai Baba, **a village holy man taken up by the Indian middle classes and made into a pan-Indian figure**, with a now global presence."
> — Hardiman 2015, Cambridge Core

**The transformation process:**

1. **Pre-1910**: Obscure Muslim fakir in rural Shirdi
2. **1910-1918**: Urban Hindu influx (Bombay middle class)
3. **1918-1936**: Death, tomb takeover, Hinduization begins
4. **1936-1956**: Narasimhaswami's propaganda (All India Sai Samaj)
5. **Post-1956**: Pan-Indian cult, millions of devotees

**Why this happened:**

> "Throughout the twentieth century, Shirdi Sai Baba devotion remained essentially a **regional movement based in Maharashtra**. But during the past several decades — at the same time that **Hindu nationalism has gained a widespread foothold** — [it expanded nationally]."
> — Cited in WRSP article on Hardiman

**Hardiman's analysis**: The cult's expansion **parallels Hindu nationalist politics**.

#### **Finding 2: The "Miracle Culture" as Rejection of Western Modernity**

**Hardiman argues**: The miracle-worker persona was **weaponized** to reject Western science while maintaining modern aspirations.

**The paradox:**

> "Although this appears to suggest that many Indians have rejected the biopolitics associated with Western modernity (as defined by Foucault), I argue that **certain elements of such biopolitics are central to this process**."
> — Hardiman 2015

**Translation**: Indians used "miracle healing" to claim **spiritual superiority over Western medicine** while **still seeking modern benefits** (hospitals, doctors, etc.).

**Sai Baba's role:**
- Promised cures without medicine
- Offered childbirth blessings (major social need)
- Provided "divine intervention" for incurable diseases
- Created dependence on supernatural rather than scientific solutions

**Hardiman's critique**: This **undermines scientific thinking** and creates a **culture of superstition**.

#### **Finding 3: Middle-Class Appropriation of a Regional Figure**

**Hardiman documents how urban middle classes transformed Sai Baba:**

**BEFORE (1858-1910):**
- Village fakir
- Served rural poor
- Obscure, local following
- Muslim identity unquestioned

**AFTER (1910-present):**
- Pan-Indian saint
- Serves urban middle class
- Global cult (millions)
- "Hindu saint" or "both Hindu and Muslim"

**The mechanism:**

> "A village holy man **taken up by the Indian middle classes** and made into a pan-Indian figure."
> — Hardiman 2015

**Why middle classes adopted him:**
1. **Miracle promises** — Easy solutions to life problems
2. **Non-threatening spirituality** — No demanding sadhana (spiritual practice)
3. **Nationalist symbol** — Indigenous vs. Western/Christian
4. **Social mobility** — Transcends caste (appeals to all)

**Result**: A **commodified saint** serving middle-class aspirations, not genuine spirituality.

#### **Finding 4: The Nationalist Discourse — "Indian Miracle" vs "Western Science"**

**Hardiman identifies a key nationalist trope:**

**The Binary:**
- **Western Science** = Materialistic, soulless, limited
- **Indian Spirituality** = Miraculous, holistic, superior

**Sai Baba became the symbol**: "Our miracles prove Indian superiority!"

**From related scholarship:**

> "Sai Baba devotion... echoes three reasons: the guarantee of **material results obtained through prayer**; the proliferation of **hagiographic books and films** on him; and Sathya Sai Baba [claiming to be] his reincarnation."
> — McLain/Warren cited in WRSP on Hardiman

**Hardiman adds a 4th reason:**

> "A **saint for a postcolonial audience** and a postcolonial discourse... integration in independent India."
> — Cited in WRSP

**Translation**: Sai Baba was **retrofitted** to serve **postcolonial nationalism** — uniting Hindus against "Western" influences.

### Hardiman's Social History Verdict

**Sai Baba's transformation represents:**

1. ✅ **Middle-class appropriation** of a regional figure
2. ✅ **Nationalist weaponization** of miracle narratives
3. ✅ **Rejection of Western science** through supernatural claims
4. ✅ **Commodification** of spirituality for urban consumption
