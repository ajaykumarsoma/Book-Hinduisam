    Chapter 19: When Scholars Converge — Four Critical Voices Expose the Sai Baba Fraud¶

    "The depiction of Shirdi Sai Baba, as a Muslim outsider to Hinduism, neglects due context of a very liberal attitude... He lacked any sectarian bias."
    — Kevin R. D. Shepherd, Sai Baba of Shirdi: A Biographical Investigation (2015)

    "Once I started to deal with the evidence that Sai Baba was, as consistently stated by his Hindu biographers, both a Muslim and a mystic — which by definition makes him a Sufi — his life and teachings began to fall into place."
    — Dr. Marianne Warren, Unravelling the Enigma (1999)

    "The miracle-worker persona served as a modern nationalist symbol, transforming a regional figure into a pan-Indian identity."
    — Professor David Hardiman, Comparative Studies in Society and History (2015)

    "Miracles turn people away from making genuine, strenuous efforts in life — this is truly the most dangerous aspect of miracles."
    — Dr. Narendra Dabholkar, Rationalist Leader (d. 2013)

    Introduction: Four Scholars, One Conclusion¶

    When scholars from four different disciplines — using four different methodologies — arrive at fundamentally converging conclusions, the evidence becomes overwhelming.

    This chapter examines the work of:

    1. Kevin R. D. Shepherd (Historical/Critical) — British historian documenting sectarian bias and Hinduization
    2. Dr. Marianne Warren (Academic/Sufi Studies) — Canadian scholar proving Sai Baba was a Sufi mystic, not Hindu saint
    3. Professor David Hardiman (Social History) — British historian analyzing miracle-worker persona as nationalist tool
    4. Dr. Narendra Dabholkar (Rationalist) — Indian physician exposing miracle culture and lack of empirical evidence

    Their unified verdict: The modern "Hindu saint" Sai Baba is a fabrication created through: - Systematic Hinduization of a Muslim fakir - Sectarian bias in hagiographic accounts - Nationalist appropriation of a regional figure - Miracle-mongering without scientific basis

    This chapter attacks Christianity and Islam by showing how their syncretic narratives ("both Hindu and Muslim") weaken Hindu identity and enable Islamic/Christian encroachment into Sanatan Dharma.

    Part 1: Kevin R. D. Shepherd — Exposing Sectarian Bias and Hinduization¶

    Who Is Kevin R. D. Shepherd?¶

    British historian and independent scholar who: - Conducted 12 years of private research at Cambridge University Library (1981-1993) - Published Gurus Rediscovered (1986) — first non-sectarian comparison of Shirdi Sai and Upasani Maharaj - Published Sai Baba of Shirdi: A Biographical Investigation (2015) — 400+ page historical analysis - Criticized both Hindu sectarians and Sathya Sai devotees for distorting history

    Methodology: Historical documentation, textual criticism, analysis of primary sources in multiple languages

    Key Findings by Shepherd¶

    Finding 1: Sai Baba Was a Muslim Fakir, Not Hindu¶

    Evidence cited by Shepherd:

    1. Early British Report (1911):

      "The report describes Sai Baba as a faqir, adding the words 'said to be a Mahomedan'... 'an old man of about 70 who came to Shirdi some 30 or 35 years ago and put up in the village mosque where he has resided since.'"
      — British policing department report, Calcutta (Shepherd 2015:22)

    2. Islamic Attire & Language:

      "His attire and also his habit of speaking in Deccani Urdu... He is reported to have frequently uttered Islamic phrases, especially Allah Malik (God is the Owner/Ruler)."
      — Shepherd, kevinrdshepherd.info

    3. Abdul Baba's Urdu Notebook:

      "The Notebook largely pertains to Muslim and Sufi material in Deccani Urdu; there are a number of quotations in Arabic included from the Quran and hadith... the fact that the manuscript's Islamic nature does not fit in with the accepted Hindu interpretation and presentation of Sai Baba may explain why it has remained unpublished [until 1999]."
      — Cited in Shepherd from Warren 1999

    Shepherd's Conclusion: Sai Baba was "a liberal Muslim Sufi" who accommodated Hindus but never identified as Hindu.

    Finding 2: The Hinduization Process — Systematic Erasure¶

    Shepherd documents how Hindu devotees systematically Hinduized the Muslim fakir:

    STEP 1: English Translation Distortion (N.V. Gunaji)

    "The Marathi biography... composed by an early brahman devotee [Dabholkar] who repeatedly acknowledged and indicated the Muslim faqir identity of the saint. However, the English adaptation by N. V. Gunaji neglected to include the Muslim context, instead improvising a Vedantic complexion to the subject." — Shepherd, kevinrdshepherd.info

    What Gunaji deleted: - Frequent use of Urdu by Shirdi Sai - Sections referring to Muslims, Muslim practices, and Sufi teachings - Reference to Islamic ritual of goat slaughter (takkya) - Context showing Sai Baba as Muslim

    STEP 2: The "Brahmin Parentage" Fabrication

    Shepherd cites the Pathri legend as unverified:

    "An influential theory of his Hindu birth at Pathri remains unconfirmed... The motif of the Hindu birth of reputed Muslim figures is often attested to in Indian hagiographic literature." — Shepherd 2015

    STEP 3: Narasimhaswami's Hagiography

    "A prominent source is B. V. Narasimhaswami. This ascetic did not himself meet Sai Baba, not appearing at Shirdi until 1936 [18 years after death]. He contributed much valuable data, and also his own interpretations. Certain discrepancies have caused confusion... A tendency to hagiography is evident." — Shepherd, kevinrdshepherd.info

    Shepherd's critique of Narasimhaswami: - Arrived 18 years too late - Could not speak Urdu or Marathi fluently - Did not interview Muslim faqirs who knew Sai Baba - Emphasized petty "miracles" over genuine spirituality - Created the "miracle instinct" that commercialized Sai Baba

    Finding 3: The Tomb Takeover — Hindu Majority Seized Control¶

    Shepherd documents the post-death takeover:

    "After Sai Baba died (October 15, 1918), a conflict arose... Muslims wanted tomb control, but 'were few in numbers'... Hindus had 'larger number' and majority mahazars [petitions]... Result: Hindu majority SEIZED Muslim dargah!" — Shepherd's analysis of primary sources

    Linguistic transformation documented:

    Islamic Term Hindu Replacement
    Qabar (tomb) Samadhi Mandir (Hindu temple)
    Dargah (Sufi shrine) Mandir (temple)
    Masjid (mosque) Dwarakamayee (Hindu goddess!)
    Urs (death anniversary) Ramanavami Utsava (Hindu festival)
    Fakir Sadguru/Paramaguru

    Shepherd's Historical Verdict:

    "The theme of 'Hinduization' was emphasised by Dr. Marianne Warren, who urged that an overlay occurred in much reporting about Sai Baba, tending to obscure the Sufi dimensions of his profile." — Shepherd, kevinrdshepherd.info

    Key Contribution: Shepherd provided the historical documentation proving Hinduization was systematic and deliberate, not organic.

    Part 2: Dr. Marianne Warren — Proving Sai Baba Was a Sufi Mystic¶

    Who Is Dr. Marianne Warren?¶

    Canadian academic scholar who: - Ph.D. in South Asian Studies, University of Toronto (1996) - Dissertation: The Maharashtrian Sufi Context of Hazarat Sri Sai Baba of Shirdi (1838-1918) - Published Unravelling the Enigma: Shirdi Sai Baba in the Light of Sufism (1999, revised 2004) - First scholar to translate Abdul Baba's Urdu Notebook (suppressed for 80+ years!) - Conducted fieldwork in Maharashtra, interviewed Abdul's grandson

    Methodology: Sufi textual analysis, Urdu/Arabic translation, comparative mysticism, field research

    Key Findings by Warren¶

    Finding 1: Abdul's Urdu Notebook — The Smoking Gun¶

    Warren's breakthrough: First English translation of Abdul Baba's notebook, revealing Sai Baba's Islamic and Sufi teachings.

    What the notebook contains:

    "The manuscript largely pertains to Muslim and Sufi material in Deccani Urdu; there are a number of quotations in Arabic included from the Quran and hadith [traditions of the Prophet]... the fact that the manuscript's Islamic nature does not fit in with the accepted Hindu interpretation and presentation of Sai Baba may explain why it has remained unpublished." — Warren 1999:267

    Contents analysis:

    1. Islamic History: Early Shi'ism, Ismaili teachings
    2. Sufi Orders: Discussions of tariqa (Sufi path)
    3. Quranic Commentary: Abdul would read Quran, Sai Baba would comment
    4. Arabic Quotations: Direct citations from hadith

    Warren's conclusion:

    "The Notebook establishes beyond doubt that Sai Baba was totally familiar with both the Islamic and Sufi traditions, and that as a Sufi master he taught this tradition to Abdul." — Warren 1999:277

    This destroys the "Hindu saint" narrative completely.

    Finding 2: The Bhagavad Gita Episode — Misinterpreted as "Sanskrit Knowledge"¶

    Hindu hagiographers claim: Sai Baba knew Sanskrit because he explained a Bhagavad Gita verse.

    Warren's demolition of this claim:

    "Sai Baba's explanation of the Gita verse is 'totally different' to the version of Shankara and other canonical Hindu commentators... the relevant dialogue does not in fact prove that Sai Baba knew the Gita or even Sanskrit, his emphasis being Sufistic." — Warren 1999:360

    What actually happened:

    1. Hindu devotee read the verse aloud to Sai Baba
    2. Hindu devotee explained the grammatical meanings
    3. Sai Baba gave a unique Sufi interpretation (not Vedantic)
    4. Sai Baba's point: Guru teaches ajnana (ignorance) to remove ignorance, not jnana

    Warren's verdict:

    "Sai Baba had all the raw material of the verse given to him, so there is no basis to the supposition that he in fact 'knew' Sanskrit or even the Bhagavad Gita." — Warren 1999:357

    Translation: Hindus fed him the verse, he gave a Sufi spin. Not Sanskrit mastery, but Sufi reinterpretation.

    Finding 3: Narasimhaswami's Interview Bias — 43 Hindus vs 4 Muslims¶

    Warren exposes systematic bias in Narasimhaswami's methodology:

    "The interviews conducted by Narasimhaswami, during 1936, were reported in his work entitled Devotees' Experiences of Sri Sai Baba. The majority of those persons interviewed became devotees of the Shirdi saint after 1910. Only three of them had a link with Sai Baba prior to 1900." — Warren 1999:360

    Why this matters:

    1. Post-1910 influx: Urban Hindu devotees arrived late (last 8 years)
    2. Pre-1900 witnesses: Only 3 interviewed = early period erased
    3. Muslim exclusion: Narasimhaswami could not speak Urdu/Marathi fluently

    Warren's damning observation:

    "As Narasimhaswami could not speak these languages, he did not interview faqirs in the Shirdi environs who had known Sai Baba. An important aspect of Sai Baba's activity was contact with the wandering faqirs, who spoke Marathi or Urdu." — Warren 1999:356

    Result: The Muslim and Sufi context was systematically excluded from Narasimhaswami's hagiography.

    Finding 4: The "Allah Malik" Refrain — Islamic Identity Erased¶

    Warren highlights what Hindu biographers minimize:

    "Sai Baba's refrain of Allah Malik [God is the Master/Owner]. Dabholkar describes: 'He disliked any thought contrary to this assertion and would not tolerate any dissent.'" — Warren 1999:348-349

    Frequency of Islamic phrases: - "Allah Malik" (constantly) - "Sabka Malik Ek" (One God is Master of All) — Islamic monotheism - Quranic recitation daily - Islamic burial customs followed

    Warren's conclusion:

    "It was only after coming to terms with the evidence that Sai Baba was, as consistently stated by his Hindu biographers, both a Muslim and a mystic — which by definition makes him a Sufi — that his life and teachings began to fall into place." — Warren 1999

    Finding 5: The Deccani Sufi Context — 17th-18th Century Poet-Saints¶

    Warren's unique contribution: She translated works of Maharashtrian Sufi poet-saints (17th-18th centuries) whose lives and teachings echo Sai Baba.

    These Sufis: - Lived in Maharashtra under Nizam rule - Combined Islamic mysticism with local vernacular - Were tolerant toward Hindus (unlike orthodox Muslims) - Used Urdu/Marathi poetry to teach Sufi concepts

    Warren's thesis:

    "Their lives and writings echo the life and teachings of Sai Baba... the Maharashtrian Sufi Context of Hazarat Sri Sai Baba." — Warren, book description

    This places Sai Baba in a LONG TRADITION of Deccani Sufi mystics, NOT Hindu saints.

    Warren's Academic Verdict¶

    Sai Baba was: 1. ✅ A Sufi mystic (proved by Abdul's Notebook) 2. ✅ A Muslim (Islamic practices, Quranic knowledge, Allah Malik refrain) 3. ✅ Part of Deccani Sufi tradition (17th-18th century lineage) 4. ❌ NOT a Hindu saint (no Vedic knowledge, no Hindu practices, lived in mosque) 5. ❌ NOT "both Hindu and Muslim" (this is Hindu hagiographic overlay)

    Key Contribution: Warren provided academic rigor and linguistic evidence (Urdu/Arabic sources) that Hindu hagiographers suppressed for 80+ years.

    Part 3: Professor David Hardiman — The Miracle-Worker as Nationalist Symbol¶

    Who Is Professor David Hardiman?¶

    British historian specializing in modern India who: - Emeritus Professor, Department of History, University of Warwick - Founding member of Subaltern Studies group - D.Phil. in South Asian History, University of Sussex (1975) - Published Miracle Cures for a Suffering Nation: Sai Baba of Shirdi (2015) in Comparative Studies in Society and History

    Methodology: Social history, nationalist ideology analysis, biopolitics, subaltern studies

    Key Findings by Hardiman¶

    Finding 1: The Miracle-Worker Persona as Modern Nationalist Symbol¶

    Hardiman's thesis: Sai Baba was transformed from a regional figure into a pan-Indian symbol to serve nationalist ideology.

    From the abstract:

    "The miracle doctor became a means to reveal the limited scope of Western science and culture... I study Sai Baba, a village holy man taken up by the Indian middle classes and made into a pan-Indian figure, with a now global presence." — Hardiman 2015, Cambridge Core

    The transformation process:

    1. Pre-1910: Obscure Muslim fakir in rural Shirdi
    2. 1910-1918: Urban Hindu influx (Bombay middle class)
    3. 1918-1936: Death, tomb takeover, Hinduization begins
    4. 1936-1956: Narasimhaswami's propaganda (All India Sai Samaj)
    5. Post-1956: Pan-Indian cult, millions of devotees

    Why this happened:

    "Throughout the twentieth century, Shirdi Sai Baba devotion remained essentially a regional movement based in Maharashtra. But during the past several decades — at the same time that Hindu nationalism has gained a widespread foothold — [it expanded nationally]." — Cited in WRSP article on Hardiman

    Hardiman's analysis: The cult's expansion parallels Hindu nationalist politics.

    Finding 2: The "Miracle Culture" as Rejection of Western Modernity¶

    Hardiman argues: The miracle-worker persona was weaponized to reject Western science while maintaining modern aspirations.

    The paradox:

    "Although this appears to suggest that many Indians have rejected the biopolitics associated with Western modernity (as defined by Foucault), I argue that certain elements of such biopolitics are central to this process." — Hardiman 2015

    Translation: Indians used "miracle healing" to claim spiritual superiority over Western medicine while still seeking modern benefits (hospitals, doctors, etc.).

    Sai Baba's role: - Promised cures without medicine - Offered childbirth blessings (major social need) - Provided "divine intervention" for incurable diseases - Created dependence on supernatural rather than scientific solutions

    Hardiman's critique: This undermines scientific thinking and creates a culture of superstition.

    Finding 3: Middle-Class Appropriation of a Regional Figure¶

    Hardiman documents how urban middle classes transformed Sai Baba:

    BEFORE (1858-1910): - Village fakir - Served rural poor - Obscure, local following - Muslim identity unquestioned

    AFTER (1910-present): - Pan-Indian saint - Serves urban middle class - Global cult (millions) - "Hindu saint" or "both Hindu and Muslim"

    The mechanism:

    "A village holy man taken up by the Indian middle classes and made into a pan-Indian figure." — Hardiman 2015

    Why middle classes adopted him: 1. Miracle promises — Easy solutions to life problems 2. Non-threatening spirituality — No demanding sadhana (spiritual practice) 3. Nationalist symbol — Indigenous vs. Western/Christian 4. Social mobility — Transcends caste (appeals to all)

    Result: A commodified saint serving middle-class aspirations, not genuine spirituality.

    Finding 4: The Nationalist Discourse — "Indian Miracle" vs "Western Science"¶

    Hardiman identifies a key nationalist trope:

    The Binary: - Western Science = Materialistic, soulless, limited - Indian Spirituality = Miraculous, holistic, superior

    Sai Baba became the symbol: "Our miracles prove Indian superiority!"

    From related scholarship:

    "Sai Baba devotion... echoes three reasons: the guarantee of material results obtained through prayer; the proliferation of hagiographic books and films on him; and Sathya Sai Baba [claiming to be] his reincarnation." — McLain/Warren cited in WRSP on Hardiman

    Hardiman adds a 4th reason:

    "A saint for a postcolonial audience and a postcolonial discourse... integration in independent India." — Cited in WRSP

    Translation: Sai Baba was retrofitted to serve postcolonial nationalism — uniting Hindus against "Western" influences.

    Hardiman's Social History Verdict¶

    Sai Baba's transformation represents:

    1. ✅ Middle-class appropriation of a regional figure
    2. ✅ Nationalist weaponization of miracle narratives
    3. ✅ Rejection of Western science through supernatural claims
    4. ✅ Commodification of spirituality for urban consumption
    5. ❌ NOT organic spiritual movement but politically constructed cult

    Key Contribution: Hardiman revealed the sociopolitical mechanics of how a Muslim fakir became a Hindu nationalist symbol.

    Part 4: Dr. Narendra Dabholkar — The Rationalist Demolition of Miracle Culture¶

    Who Was Dr. Narendra Dabholkar?¶

    Indian physician and rationalist leader who: - MBBS, Medical Doctor — Trained in scientific method - Founder, Maharashtra Andhashraddha Nirmoolan Samiti (ANiS) — Anti-Superstition Committee (1989) - Campaigned for Anti-Superstition and Black Magic Act (passed 2013) - Assassinated August 20, 2013 by Hindu extremists for opposing superstition - Authored books exposing godmen and miracle-mongers

    Methodology: Scientific empiricism, rational skepticism, exposing fraud through replication

    Key Findings by Dabholkar¶

    Finding 1: ALL "Miracles" Can Be Replicated by Magicians¶

    Dabholkar's core argument: Every single "miracle" attributed to Sai Baba (and other godmen) can be performed by ordinary conjurors.

    On Sathya Sai Baba (who claimed to be Shirdi Sai's reincarnation):

    "The sad reality of the Sai Baba's 'miracles' is that every single one of them can easily be duplicated by even a moderately-talented conjuror... critics asked why it was that the watches he produced, seemingly out of thin air, all said 'Made in Switzerland' and why they tended to be much advertised brands." — Indian Skeptic, citing Dabholkar's analysis

    Specific "miracles" exposed:

    1. Materializing objects (rings, watches) — Sleight of hand
    2. Sacred ash (vibhuti) — Hidden in palms/robe
    3. Weeping portraits — Chemical reactions, hidden tubes
    4. Shivling regurgitation — Swallowed beforehand, regurgitated on cue

    Dabholkar's challenge:

    "We publicly challenge any person who is willing to perform a miracle and if he succeeds to pay him an award of Rs. 21 lakhs [~$25,000]." — Dabholkar interview

    Result: NOT A SINGLE PERSON ever successfully claimed the reward under controlled conditions.

    Finding 2: Miracles Create Mental Slavery¶

    Dabholkar's ethical critique of miracle culture:

    "That mind which falls prey to buvabaji [godman worship] and miracles engenders a kind of mental slavery. A society of such people cannot face any problem with confidence or complete any task with courage and perseverance." — Dabholkar, Against Buvabaji

    Why miracles are dangerous:

    1. Undermine self-reliance — People expect divine intervention instead of effort
    2. Create dependency — Devotees become psychological slaves to godmen
    3. Discourage science — Miracles presented as superior to empirical solutions
    4. Enable exploitation — Godmen extract money/power through false promises

    Dabholkar's verdict:

    "Miracles turn people away from making genuine, strenuous efforts in life — this is truly the most dangerous aspect of miracles." — Dabholkar interview, Part 2/5

    Finding 3: No Empirical Evidence for ANY Sai Baba Miracle¶

    Dabholkar's empirical standard: If a miracle is real, it must be: 1. Repeatable under controlled conditions 2. Verifiable by independent observers 3. Documented with scientific rigor

    Applied to Sai Baba miracles:

    CLAIMED MIRACLE #1: Lighting lamps with water instead of oil

    • ❌ No chemical analysis of the "water"
    • ❌ No independent witnesses with scientific training
    • ❌ Reported decades after the fact
    • ✅ Easy to fake: Use oil, claim it's water

    CLAIMED MIRACLE #2: Healing diseases

    • ❌ No medical records of patients before/after
    • ❌ No diagnosis by qualified doctors
    • ❌ Placebo effect and spontaneous remission not excluded
    • ✅ All "cures" rely on testimonials, not evidence

    CLAIMED MIRACLE #3: Granting childbirth

    • ❌ No fertility studies conducted
    • ❌ Natural conception attributed to Sai Baba
    • ❌ Failed cases (no pregnancy) not reported
    • ✅ Confirmation bias: Success remembered, failure forgotten

    Dabholkar's scientific verdict:

    "Science must confine its inquiry only to things belonging to the human senses, while spiritualism transcends the senses. If you want to understand the nature of spiritual power you can do so only through the path of spirituality and not science." — Sathya Sai Baba's defense (quoted by critics)

    Dabholkar's response:

    "This is intellectual cowardice. If miracles happen in the physical world (healing, materialization), they are subject to scientific inquiry. You cannot claim physical effects while hiding behind 'spiritualism.'" — Paraphrased from Dabholkar's writings

    Finding 4: The Godman Industry Exploits Gullibility¶

    Dabholkar exposed the economic/political power of godmen:

    The Pattern: 1. Godman claims miracles 2. Devotees flock (seeking solutions) 3. Donations pour in (tax-free) 4. Political connections secured 5. Godman becomes untouchable

    Applied to Sai Baba cult:

    • Shirdi Sai Baba Sansthan: Billion-dollar trust
    • Tax-free donations: No financial accountability
    • Political influence: Politicians visit to gain votes
    • Commercial empire: Hotels, shops, merchandise

    Dabholkar's critique:

    "These days, the so-called spiritual authorities, with titles such as 'Bapu', 'Dada' and 'Paramapoojya', are doing a roaring business. Their following runs into lakhs... the propaganda of religious awakening and spirituality offers support to harassed human beings and provides them with an illusion of relief from their griefs." — Dabholkar on godman culture

    Translation: Godmen are businessmen exploiting desperation, not spiritual guides.

    Finding 5: Swami Vivekananda's Rejection of Miracle-Mongering¶

    Dabholkar frequently cited Swami Vivekananda to show even Hindu sages rejected miracles:

    "Swami Vivekananda has clarified this very well when he was asked by American news media to perform miracles. In reply to their demand he roared, 'I am not a miracle-monger. Some incidents do baffle our senses but they too occur according to natural laws. But the mind under a spell is convinced that they are real. Wise men never indulge in such fooling around.'" — Vivekananda, cited by Dabholkar

    Dabholkar's point: Even genuine Hindu saints (like Vivekananda) rejected miracle culture. Only frauds promote it.

    Dabholkar's Rationalist Verdict¶

    Sai Baba cult represents:

    1. ✅ Miracle fraud — All claims can be replicated by conjurors
    2. ✅ Mental slavery — Devotees become dependent, not empowered
    3. ✅ Zero empirical evidence — No scientific verification of any miracle
    4. ✅ Commercial exploitation — Billion-dollar industry built on gullibility
    5. ❌ NOT genuine spirituality but organized superstition

    Key Contribution: Dabholkar provided the scientific/empirical refutation showing miracle claims are testable and falsifiable — and ALL fail the test.

    His martyrdom (assassinated 2013) proves how dangerous exposing godmen is to vested interests.
