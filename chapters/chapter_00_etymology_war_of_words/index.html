    Part 1: The Myth of the Word "God"¶

    1.1: The Etymology — "God" Originally Meant "Idol"¶

    Most people assume "God" is a universal, neutral term for the Supreme Creator.

    This is FALSE.

    The Proto-Germanic Root: Gudą¶

    Etymology:
    The word "God" originates from the Proto-Germanic gudą, which traces back to the Proto-Indo-European root *ǵʰeu̯-, meaning:

    1. "To pour" or "to invoke" (as in a libation ritual)
    2. "An invoked object" (the recipient of offerings)

    Academic Source:

    "The neuter noun gudą was used in pre-Christian times to refer to an 'idol' or a 'libated object of worship.'"
    — Guus Kroonen, Etymological Dictionary of Proto-Germanic (Brill, 2013), p. 194

    Oxford English Dictionary:

    "The word 'God' is of Germanic origin, from gudą, originally a pagan term for a deity invoked during sacrificial rites."

    What This Means:¶

    In ancient Germanic and Pagan European traditions, "God" (Gudą) was:

    ✅ A proper noun for a specific tribal deity
    ✅ A physical object (wooden/stone pillar) used as a focal point for ritual offerings
    ✅ Identical in function to a Hindu Grama Devata (village deity) or Murti (consecrated form)

    A wooden or stone pillar carved with the face of a tribal protector, used as a focal point for pouring ritual offerings—IDENTICAL in function to a Grama Devata or a Murti.

    1.2: The Linguistic Hijack — How Christianity Stole the Word¶

    When Germanic and Pagan European tribes were forcibly converted to Christianity (c. 300-1000 CE), missionaries faced a problem:

    Problem: These tribes had no word for the abstract, singular, wrathful "Creator-Judge" of the Bible.

    Solution: The Church repurposed the existing word Gudą (their local idol/deity) and applied it to the Biblical Yahweh/Jehovah.

    Result: Linguistic identity theft.

    The Contradiction of Scale¶

    Original Gudą (Pre-Christian) Biblical "God" (Post-Christian)
    Local, tribal deity Universal, singular Creator
    Tangible object (wood/stone idol) Abstract, invisible entity
    Invoked by humans (we call upon it) Commands humans (it orders us)
    Pluralistic (one of many gods) Exclusive ("no other gods before me")
    Protector/Helper Judge/Punisher

    The Sleight of Hand:
    By using the word "God" to describe the Absolute Creator, Christianity performed a linguistic sleight of hand:

    They took a word that meant "an object we care for"
    And used it to describe "a Being that demands total fear."

    From "Invoked" to "Commander"¶

    The PIE root *ǵʰeu̯- (to invoke/call upon) implies that: - The human is the active agent seeking connection - The deity is responsive to human invocation

    In the Biblical/Quranic context, the roles are REVERSED: - "God" is the active commander - The human is the passive slave (Arabic: 'Abd)

    This is a 180° inversion of the original meaning.

    1.3: The Identity Theft — Biblical "God" is Not the Creator¶

    Critical Analysis:

    In the Bible and Quran, you do NOT see a neutral "common noun" for a Supreme Reality.

    You see proper names of tribal characters:

    Hebrew: El / Elohim¶

    Etymology:
    - El (אֵל): From the Northwest Semitic root 'l = "power" or "ruler" - Originally a proper name for the head of the Canaanite pantheon (El Elyon)

    Biblical Evidence:
    - Psalm 82:1 — "Elohim stands in the assembly of El" (polytheistic context!) - Genesis 14:18-22 — Melchizedek, priest of El Elyon (God Most High)

    Scholarly Source:

    "In Ugaritic texts (c. 1400 BCE), El is the supreme deity of the Canaanite pantheon, father of Ba'al and other gods."
    — Mark S. Smith, The Early History of God: Yahweh and the Other Deities in Ancient Israel (Eerdmans, 2002)

    What This Proves:
    "Elohim" is a plural form (gods) and originally referred to a council of deities, not a singular Creator.

    Arabic: Allah (الله)¶

    Etymology:
    - Allah = Al-Ilah (The + Deity) - Ilah (إله) is the Arabic cognate of Hebrew El - Literally means "THE Deity" (of a specific tribe)

    Pre-Islamic Evidence:
    - Al-Lāt, Al-'Uzzā, Manāt = "The daughters of Allah" (Quran 53:19-20, "Satanic Verses") - Allah was one god among 360 in the pre-Islamic Kaaba

    Academic Source:

    "The name 'Allah' predates Islam and was used by pre-Islamic Arabs to refer to a high god within a polytheistic pantheon."
    — Encyclopedia Britannica, "Allah"

    What This Proves:
    Allah is NOT a universal term for "God" — it's a proper name of a specific Arabian deity who was promoted to monotheism by Muhammad.
