    Das Kapital: Logical Fallacies and Economic Nonsense¶

    Marx's magnum opus is built on a foundation of logical fallacies that economists have refuted for over a century.

    Fallacy #1: The Labor Theory of Value (LTV)¶

    Marx's Claim:

    "The value of a commodity is determined by the socially necessary labor time required to produce it."

    The Refutation:

    Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk (Austrian economist, 1896):

    "Marx's labor theory of value is logically incoherent. If value comes only from labor, then why do diamonds (little labor) cost more than water (abundant but laborious to transport)? Subjective utility, not labor time, determines value." — Karl Marx and the Close of His System (1896)

    Modern Consensus:

    "The Labor Theory of Value faces the problem of joint production: if a production process produces more than one commodity, how does one calculate socially necessary labor time? Some labor values can be undefined, nil, or negative." — Ian Steedman, economist (cited in multiple academic sources)

    "Modern fiat money utterly refutes Marx's labor theory of value, because money needs to be a produced commodity with a labor value for his theory to work. But money has long since ceased to be a produced commodity and is now fiat money." — Social Democracy for the 21st Century blog

    Why This Matters:

    Without the LTV, Marx's entire theory of "exploitation" collapses. If labor doesn't create all value, then profit isn't "stolen surplus value" — it's compensation for capital, risk, and entrepreneurship.

    Fallacy #2: The Transformation Problem (Marx's Self-Contradiction)¶

    The Problem:

    In Volume 1 of Das Kapital, Marx claims:

    Commodities exchange at their labor values.

    In Volume 3, Marx admits:

    Commodities actually exchange at prices of production (which differ from labor values due to capital intensity).

    The Contradiction:

    Böhm-Bawerk:

    "The beginning of any theoretical system has probably never been belittled more thoroughly and conclusively by its ending. Marx's Volume 3 refutes Volume 1."

    Why This Matters:

    Marx spent decades writing a theory that contradicts itself. If prices don't reflect labor values (as he admits in Volume 3), then his entire theory of exploitation (Volume 1) is invalid.
